UX Laws

Jakob's Law

Users spend most of their time on other sites, so they expect your site to work similarly. This means:

  • Design intuitively, matching users' mental models.
  • Utilize familiar layouts and navigation patterns.

Example: A user familiar with the layout of popular e-commerce sites will expect similar navigation on your site.

Fitts's Law

This law states that the time required to move to a target area is a function of the distance to the target and the size of the target. Key points include:

  • Buttons should be large enough to click easily (minimum target size: 44x44 px).
  • Place buttons in easily accessible areas.

Example: A large "Buy Now" button at the bottom of a product page is easier to click than a small one tucked away in a corner.

Hick's Law

This law suggests that the time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices. To apply this:

  • Simplify choices by breaking down complex tasks into smaller steps.
  • Highlight recommended options to guide users.

Example: A checkout process that asks for shipping information first, then payment details, reduces cognitive load.

Miller's Law

The average person can hold about 7 Â± 2 items in their working memory. To reduce cognitive load:

  • Limit the amount of information presented at once.
  • Chunk information into smaller, manageable units.

Example: Grouping related items in a menu helps users navigate more easily.

Postel's Law

This law emphasizes being tolerant of user input. Key practices include:

  • Provide clear feedback for user actions.
  • Be flexible with user errors and guide them back on track.

Example: If a user enters an incorrect format for a phone number, provide a gentle prompt to correct it.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

Users often perceive more aesthetically pleasing designs as more usable. To leverage this:

  • Invest in good visual design to enhance user perception.
  • Use attractive layouts to mask minor usability issues.

Example: A beautifully designed app may receive more positive feedback, even if it has some usability flaws.

Von Restorff Effect

This effect states that an item that stands out is more likely to be remembered. To apply this:

  • Make important information visually distinctive.
  • Use restraint to avoid overwhelming users with too many highlights.

Example: Highlighting a special offer in a bright color can draw attention and increase conversions.

Doherty Threshold

This law states that users need feedback within 400 ms to maintain their attention. To implement this:

  • Provide immediate feedback for user actions.
  • Use animations or progress indicators to make waiting tolerable.

Example: A loading animation during data retrieval keeps users engaged and reduces perceived wait time.

Combining UX Laws

Understanding how these laws interact can enhance user experience:

  • Jakob's Law + Fitts's Law: By designing familiar layouts with large, easily clickable buttons, users can navigate intuitively.
  • Hick's Law + Miller's Law: Simplifying choices while limiting information overload helps users make decisions quickly and confidently.
  • Doherty Threshold + Aesthetic-Usability Effect: Providing quick feedback in an attractive interface can enhance user satisfaction and trust.
